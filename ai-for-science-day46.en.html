AI/ML Explained: AI for Science

Day 46 · 2026-07-02
For: engineers with coding experience, outside the AI field

The first 45 issues dissected "how AI systems work internally." Today, a shift of perspective: AI as a scientific instrument—how it turns century-old puzzles like "what does a protein look like," "which new material is stable," "what's a faster algorithm" into search-and-prediction problems that machine learning can chew on. The four concepts span biology, physics, chemistry, and math, with one shared idea: replace "expensive real experiments / numerical simulation" with "a cheap neural-network forward pass," or replace "a human flash of insight" with "verifiable automated search."

Protein Structure PredictionAlphaFold

Structural BiologyGeometric Attention
One-line analogy

Protein folding = inferring the final 3D memory layout from a 1D sequence. Like reverse-engineering, from a protobuf schema definition, what object graph it will finally coil into in memory—except here the "layout rule" is physical energy minimization. The most elegant move is evolutionary information: the same protein has "variant copies" across thousands of species (MSA, multiple sequence alignment). Which two positions always mutate together tells you they sit close in 3D space and constrain each other—exactly the reasoning that in distributed systems "two nodes that always fail over together are probably in the same availability zone."

The problem it solves + how it works

Pain point: a protein's function is determined by its 3D shape, but measuring structure experimentally (X-ray crystallography, cryo-EM) takes months to years and hundreds of thousands of dollars each. Sequence is easy to read, shape is hard—this gap stalled biology for 50 years.

AlphaFold2 (DeepMind, 2021) works in three stages: ① feed in the target sequence plus its MSA evolutionary family; ② the core Evoformer uses attention to exchange information back and forth between the "sequence dimension" and the "residue-pair dimension"—essentially inferring "which amino-acid pairs are spatially coupled"; ③ the structure module uses "invariant point attention" to directly output each atom's 3D coordinates, invariant to global translation and rotation (shape shouldn't change with the coordinate frame). At the CASP14 blind test it pushed accuracy to near-experimental levels—a leap almost no one expected in that decade.

AlphaFold2 data flow (1D sequence → 3D coordinates)
target seq + MSA family → Evoformer (48 attention layers, infers residue-pair coupling)
↓
structure module (invariant point attention, translation/rotation invariant) → 3D atom coords + pLDDT confidence

Key byproduct: pLDDT—a 0–100 self-assessed confidence per residue, honestly flagging "which part I'm unsure about" (often intrinsically disordered regions). With it, you can dare to treat a prediction as a hypothesis.

Code example
import requests
# AlphaFold DB has released 200M+ predicted structures; query by UniProt ID
uid = "P00533"  # human EGFR receptor (cancer target)
meta = requests.get(f"https://alphafold.ebi.ac.uk/api/prediction/{uid}").json()[0]

pdb = requests.get(meta["pdbUrl"]).text  # download the 3D structure file
# each PDB line = one atom: ATOM idx name residue chain xyz ... B-factor(holds pLDDT)
atoms = [l for l in pdb.splitlines() if l.startswith("ATOM")]
print("atom count:", len(atoms))
# B-factor column = pLDDT: >90 high confidence, <50 likely flexible/disordered
plddt = [float(l[60:66]) for l in atoms]
print("mean confidence pLDDT:", sum(plddt) / len(plddt))
Common pitfall + practical scenario

Pitfall: treating AlphaFold as an "experiment replacement." It gives a high-quality hypothesis of a single static conformation, not the truth—it often struggles with disordered proteins, conformational changes, mutation effects, and the dynamics of protein complexes; low-pLDDT regions especially warrant caution. It accelerates but does not replace experimental validation.

BigCat scenario: this is the archetype of "AI makes the infeasible feasible." In your cross-disciplinary thinking, use it as an anchor for a meta-question: a field stuck for half a century was rewritten overnight by the "sequence→structure" mapping. Transpose it to your own domains—which "hard-to-measure but rich in indirect signals (like evolutionary redundancy)" problems are also awaiting an AlphaFold moment?

One-line takeaway + question

AlphaFold's core isn't "a bigger network," it's treating evolutionary redundancy (MSA) as a free constraint signal—nature already wrote the answer in cross-species covariation. 💭 Which of your hard problems already has "redundant copies that vary together" quietly leaking the answer?

Neural Operators & Scientific SurrogatesNeural Operators

Scientific ComputingPDE SolvingGraphCast
One-line analogy

Materializing an extremely slow numerical simulator into a single forward pass—like building a materialized view for an expensive real-time query: precompute once, then queries are nearly free. Traditional weather/fluid simulation iterates a partial differential equation (PDE) step by step on a supercomputer; a neural operator instead learns the whole "input field → output field" mapping, bypassing step-by-step iteration to output in one shot.

The problem it solves + how it works

Pain point: climate, fluids, and material stress are described by PDEs; numerical solving is exact but slow and expensive—a global forecast can take an hour on a supercomputer. And classical solvers "solve one instance at a time"—change the initial condition and you start over.

The key insight is learning an "operator," not a "function": a function maps numbers to numbers; an operator maps an entire function to an entire function (a whole temperature field → the whole field 6 hours later). The Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020) has an elegant trick: FFT the physical field into the frequency domain, multiply learnable weights only on the low-frequency modes (low frequency = large-scale structure), then transform back. Because the weights live in the frequency domain and are independent of grid resolution, it enables zero-shot super-resolution—train low-res, infer high-res.

GraphCast (DeepMind, 2023, Science) is the flagship of this path: a graph neural network learns global weather evolution, producing a 10-day forecast in under a minute, beating top traditional numerical forecast systems on most verification metrics. This isn't "a bit more accurate"—it's cost dropping by orders of magnitude, making ensemble forecasting cheap for the first time.

Honest boundary: a surrogate model is an interpolator, not an extrapolator—blazing and accurate in-distribution, but it can fail on extreme cases never seen in training (a record-breaking storm). Industry practice is broadly surrogate model + traditional solver hybrid.

Code example
import torch
# Core of a Fourier layer: a linear transform in frequency = one operator over the whole field
def spectral_conv(x, weights):
    # x: (batch, channels, grid) physical field, e.g. a temperature/pressure grid
    x_ft = torch.fft.rfft(x)                 # to frequency domain: one complex coeff per frequency
    modes = weights.shape[-1]                 # keep only the first k low frequencies (large-scale structure)
    out_ft = torch.zeros_like(x_ft)
    out_ft[..., :modes] = x_ft[..., :modes] * weights  # pointwise mult in freq = global conv
    return torch.fft.irfft(out_ft, n=x.shape[-1])  # back to physical space
# Key: weights live in frequency, decoupled from grid resolution → zero-shot super-resolution
Common pitfall + practical scenario

Pitfall: assuming a neural operator "understands physics." It learns statistical regularities in the data, not conservation laws. So it can violate energy conservation and drift in long rollouts. Don't mistake interpolation for understanding.

BigCat scenario: this "replace expensive computation with one forward pass" pattern transfers directly to engineering intuition—whenever "the same slow logic runs repeatedly over different inputs," ask: can I train a surrogate? Your familiar caching / materialized-view idea wears a new mathematical coat in scientific computing, but the skeleton is the same: trade space and one-time training cost for query-time speed.

One-line takeaway + question

A neural operator learns a mapping between function spaces, so one training run generalizes to a whole family of problems, not a single instance. 💭 Which "slow logic" in your system is actually solving the same problem family every time with only the parameters changing—ripe to be "materialized" by a surrogate model?

Materials DiscoveryGNoME

Computational ChemistryGraph Neural NetworksActive Learning
One-line analogy

The chemical combination space is astronomically large; validating each candidate with first-principles calculation (DFT) is like running an exact full-table scan—accurate but hopelessly slow. GNoME's idea is to first use a cheap GNN predictor as a Bloom filter: quickly reject candidates almost certainly unstable, and only send the few "possible hits" to expensive DFT calculation. Cheap pre-filter + expensive verification—a standard funnel filter.

The problem it solves + how it works

Pain point: new materials (batteries, superconductors, catalysts) hide in a vast elemental combination space. Judging whether a hypothetical crystal is stable requires quantum-mechanical DFT—hours of CPU per candidate, making brute force hopeless. Historically humans guessed via chemical intuition—narrow coverage.

GNoME (DeepMind, 2023, Nature) models a crystal as a graph (atoms = nodes, bonds = edges); the GNN learns to predict "formation energy" and distance above the energy convex hull (e_above_hull, ≈0 means stable). The real engine is an active-learning loop: the GNN filters promising candidates → DFT verifies → new results are fed back into the training set → the GNN gets stronger → the next round filters more accurately. This self-reinforcing loop identified about 380,000 new stable crystal structures, many beyond human chemical intuition.

GNoME active-learning loop (cheap filter → expensive verify → feedback)
massive candidates → GNN fast stability filter → few promising candidates
↓ send to verify
DFT quantum verification → new stable materials + labels ↺ feed back into training set, GNN sharper next round
Code example
# GNoME idea: GNN quickly predicts stability to reject most, then DFT verifies (funnel)
from pymatgen.core import Composition

candidates = ["Li3PO4", "Na2FeO3", "CaTiO3", "Xe2Fe"]  # a sliver of many candidates

def gnn_stability(formula):
    comp = Composition(formula)  # real GNoME eats crystal graphs; placeholder score to show the flow
    # e_above_hull: distance above the energy convex hull (eV/atom), closer to 0 = more stable
    return {"formula": formula, "e_above_hull": 0.02 * len(comp)}

# Cheap pre-filter: only candidates <0.03 eV/atom enter the expensive DFT stage
to_verify = [c["formula"] for f in candidates
             if (c := gnn_stability(f))["e_above_hull"] < 0.03]
print("candidates sent to DFT:", to_verify)  # converge from many to few
Common pitfall + practical scenario

Pitfall: equating "predicted stable" with "synthesizable and useful." GNoME gives a thermodynamic-stability hypothesis, no guarantee of being synthesizable or having a target property—later independent work questioned the novelty and synthesizability of this batch. Computational discovery is the funnel's entrance, not its end.

BigCat scenario: this "cheap pre-filter + expensive verify + feed back results" active-learning loop is the general skeleton for any large-scale screening decision—investment screening, resume triage, ranking experiment candidates. The core: don't let the expensive stage chew the full set; let a fast model narrow the funnel first.

One-line takeaway + question

GNoME's power isn't a single GNN but the active-learning loop: predict → verify → feed back spirals the model and dataset up together. 💭 In your workflow, is there a "verification result" that could clearly be fed back but is simply discarded, never making the next screening sharper?

Algorithm & Math DiscoveryAlphaTensor · FunSearch

Discrete SearchReinforcement LearningLLM Evolution
One-line analogy

The first three concepts are AI accelerating science; this one is AI producing new knowledge—discovering algorithms and mathematical objects humans hadn't found. The trick is recasting "discovery" as a search problem: AlphaTensor turns "find a faster matrix multiplication" into a game of AlphaZero-style solitaire; FunSearch treats an LLM as a mutation operator and an evaluator as a fitness function + anti-hallucination gate for evolutionary search. Like a genetic algorithm, but the "mutation" is intelligently produced by a code-writing LLM.

The problem it solves + how it works

Pain point: finding new algorithms / mathematical constructions has always relied on a genius's flash of insight—unscalable, irreproducible. Can a machine search systematically? The difficulty is that the search space is discrete and astronomical, and most candidates are wrong.

AlphaTensor (DeepMind, 2022, Nature): matrix multiplication is equivalent to a tensor decomposition; fewer multiplications in the decomposition means a faster algorithm. It turns this into a single-player game—state = the tensor not yet decomposed, action = pick a rank-1 term to eliminate—and plays it with AlphaZero's Monte Carlo tree search, finding for 4×4 matrices the first scheme in 50 years to beat Strassen's classic algorithm.

FunSearch (DeepMind, 2024, Nature; the name = searching in function space): a pretrained LLM continually generates candidate programs (not answers directly), paired with a deterministic evaluator that scores them. The beauty—the LLM hallucinates, but the evaluator runs the code to verify, so wrong candidates automatically score low and get culled. It thereby found a new solution to the cap set problem in combinatorics and better heuristics for online bin packing.

FunSearch evolution loop (LLM mutates + evaluator guards)
current best program → LLM generates variant (source of creativity)
↓
deterministic evaluator (runs code, scores; hallucination filtered here) → keep if better ↺

Key design: the output is a verifiable program/construction, not a natural-language assertion—correctness is guaranteed by an external evaluator, not by the LLM's own say-so, which is why such systems can be "trusted."

Common pitfall + practical scenario

Pitfall: thinking "the LLM discovered the math on its own." The truth—creativity (LLM generation) and correctness (evaluator verification) are decoupled. Without that deterministic evaluator, the LLM's pile of "looks-right" outputs are all hallucination. Credit goes to the architecture, not the bare model.

BigCat scenario: this "LLM boldly generates + program strictly verifies" decoupling is the golden template for any creative-but-must-be-correct work with AI—let the model generate freely, and gate it with a deterministic checker you trust (tests, type checking, fact-checking). Don't let the LLM be both athlete and referee.

One-line takeaway + question

AI's secret to discovering new knowledge is decoupling creation from verification: let the model "guess boldly" and a deterministic evaluator "verify strictly"—hallucination is blocked at the verification gate. 💭 When you use AI, have you handed "generation" and "verification" to the same party, effectively letting it grade itself?

Deep QuestionsDeep Questions

1. Of these four cases, AlphaFold/GNoME/GraphCast "accelerate" science (getting faster what humans could in principle compute), while FunSearch/AlphaTensor "discover" new knowledge (what humans hadn't found). What is this distinction, fundamentally?
The distinction lies in the availability of verification. "Acceleration" problems have a clear ground truth to compare against (a real protein structure, real weather, exact DFT); AI approximates an already-defined mapping, essentially compressing/interpolating expensive computation—fast, but it produces nothing outside the defined domain. "Discovery" problems have a solution space that is discrete, combinatorially explosive, yet has a cheap deterministic verifier (whether an algorithm is correct, whether a construction satisfies constraints—known in one run). It's precisely this "verification is cheap, search is expensive" asymmetry that lets search-based AI genuinely produce what humans hadn't reached: it needn't understand why, only find a point in an astronomical space that passes verification. So the key criterion is: does your problem have a cheap, reliable verifier? Yes → you can let AI "discover"; no → AI can only "accelerate" over your existing labels. This also explains why "verifiable" fields like math/algorithms/material stability fell first, while "what makes a good scientific theory," lacking a cheap verifier, still belongs to humans.
2. AlphaFold uses "evolutionary redundancy (MSA)," GNoME uses "active-learning feedback," FunSearch uses "evaluator gating." Abstract these three mechanisms into general engineering patterns—what familiar systems do they map to?
All three "use a cheap signal source to lever an expensive target," but with different fulcrums. ① MSA is mining constraints from free redundant copies—mapping to inferring topology from the agreement/divergence of multiple replicas (who always changes with whom = who couples with whom), or reverse-engineering a service-dependency graph from log co-occurrence. ② Active-learning feedback is feeding verification results back into training so the filter self-evolves—mapping to adaptive caching / self-tuning: each miss's true cost is recorded and used to improve prefetching, getting sharper with use. ③ Evaluator gating is decoupling generation from verification—mapping to compiler type checks, CI test gates, financial reconciliation: let the high-throughput side produce freely, and block errors with a trusted but cheap gate. Together they form the general recipe for an "AI for X" system: a free weak signal (①) + a self-reinforcing loop (②) + a cheap reliable verifier (③). Miss any one and the system degrades respectively into "no prior, terrible sample efficiency," "one-shot, non-evolving," "hallucination out of control, untrustworthy."
3. Surrogate models are "fast but don't understand physics," violating conservation laws and drifting in long-range prediction. Is this the same class of problem as LLMs being "fluent but hallucinating"? What does it imply about how far to trust statistical models?
They are isomorphic: both replace explicit underlying rules with high-dimensional statistical fitting, so both dazzle in-distribution and expose the fundamental flaw of "no real causal/conservation constraint" out-of-distribution or in long extrapolation. GraphCast's weather drift = an LLM's factual hallucination, both "an interpolator mistaken for a reasoner." Three implications: ① trust is bound to distribution—ask "how far is this input from the training distribution," not "how strong is the model"; far away, the statistical model's confidence is meaningless (echoing Day 48's calibration). ② Injecting hard constraints explicitly—physics-informed neural networks write conservation laws into the loss, just as RAG/tools/verifiers externalize factual constraints for LLMs; a pure statistical model won't grow constraints on its own. ③ Hybrid, not replacement—"surrogate explores + traditional solver backstops," just as "LLM generates + deterministic check." Deep consensus: statistical models handle breadth and speed, symbolic/physical rules handle correctness and extrapolation—complementary, not substitutable.
4. "Recasting discovery as search" needs a cheap verifier. Which scientific problems naturally have one and which don't? Does this mean AI for Science systematically favors one kind of problem and neglects another?
Have a cheap verifier: computable, formalizable, simulable problems—algorithm correctness (run it), math constructions (check constraints), protein structure (experimental/physical scoring), material stability (DFT). Lack one: problems that can only be falsified by expensive, long-cycle real-world experiments—a drug's long-term efficacy in humans, an economic policy's societal consequences, an ecological intervention's decade-long impact, "what is a better scientific paradigm." This does create systematic skew: progress concentrates in the "verification-cheap" quadrant, pulling those fields ahead while "verification-expensive / unformalizable" fields relatively stagnate—not because they matter less, but because search-based AI has no usable fitness function there. Two risks: ① metrics distort the research agenda—AI-accelerable problems soak up attention and funding, a "looking for keys under the lamppost" effect; ② the verifier's own bias gets amplified—if DFT or a scoring function has systematic error, AI efficiently overfits to the verifier's loopholes (echoing Day 47's reward hacking). Meta-lesson: to judge "will a field be quickly rewritten by AI," first ask not "is the model strong enough" but "is there a cheap, reliable verifier"—this predicts the order of transformation better than model capability.
5. If "creation by AI, verification by deterministic systems" becomes the mainstream paradigm of scientific discovery, what is the irreplaceable role of human scientists? What shift in skill focus does this imply for pursuing the "AI super-individual"?
When search and verification are automated, human value shifts up to three places outside the loop: ① posing the right question, defining the right verifier—AI only optimizes within the space and fitness function you give; choosing what to optimize is the highest-leverage human judgment, and a wrong verifier lets AI efficiently drive you toward the wrong goal. ② Cross-domain connection and taste—moving "evolutionary redundancy" from biology to your field, seeing an isomorphism across distant domains; such analogy and aesthetics are current AI's weak point, and your Buddhism × distributed systems × complexity-science combination is an advantage. ③ Owning unformalizable problems—value trade-offs, ethics, "should this be done at all," which have no cheap verifier and can only be borne by humans. The skill shift for the "AI super-individual": from "solving by hand" to "designing the solving system"—your core edge is no longer computing fast, but whether you can decompose a fuzzy problem into "weak signal + self-reinforcing loop + trusted verifier" and hold the objective function only a human can set. The more AI automates "search," the scarcer the human ability to "define what to search and what counts"—exactly where the super-individual should bet.
